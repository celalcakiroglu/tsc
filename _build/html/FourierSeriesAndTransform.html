Fourier Series and the Fourier Transform¶

Fourier Series¶

The Fourier series representation of a periodic discrete-time signal \(x(n)\) is defined as

(1)\[x(n)=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}c_k e^{j2\pi kn/N}\]

where \(N\) is the fundamental period of \(x(n)\). In equation (1), \(c_k\) denotes the Fourier coefficients of \(x(n)\) and they represent the frequency components contained in \(x(n)\). For each \(k \in \lbrace 0, 1, ..., N-1 \rbrace\), \(c_k\) can be calculated as

(2)\[c_k=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)e^{-j2\pi kn/N}\]

Equation (2) can be derived applying the following 4 steps:

  • Step 1: Both sides of equation (1) are multiplied by \(e^{-j2\pi ln/N}\), where \(l \in \lbrace 0, 1, ..., N-1 \rbrace\).
(3)\[\begin{split}x(n) e^{-j2\pi ln/N} & = \sum _{k=0}^{N-1} c_k e^{j 2\pi (k-l) n/N } \\ & = \sum _{k=0}^{N-1} c_k \Big\lbrace e^{j2\pi (k-l)/N}\Big\rbrace^n \\\end{split}\]
  • Step 2: Let \(r=e^{j2\pi (k-l)/N}\) and sum both sides of equation (3) over the index \(n\)
(4)\[\sum _{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)e^{-j2\pi ln/N} = \sum _{k=0}^{N-1} c_k \sum _{n=0}^{N-1} r^n\]
  • Step 3: Using the geometric series summation:
\[\begin{split} \begin{equation*} \sum _{n=0}^{N-1} r^n= \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} N & \mbox{ if $ k=l$}\\ \displaystyle\frac{1-r^N}{1-r} & \mbox{ if $k \neq l$} \end{array} \right. = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} N & \mbox{ if $ k=l$}\\ \displaystyle\frac{1- e^{j2\pi (k-l)} }{1-e^{j2\pi (k-l)/N}} & \mbox{ if $k \neq l$} \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}e^{j2\pi (k-l)}=\cos{2\pi (k-l)} + j\sin{2\pi (k-l)}=1 \Rightarrow \sum _{n=0}^{N-1} r^n= \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} N & \mbox{ if $ k=l$}\\ 0 & \mbox{ if $k \neq l$} \end{array} \right.\end{split}\]
  • Step 4: Plugging the result of step 3 in equation (4) we obtain:
\[\sum _{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)e^{-j2\pi ln/N} = c_0 \cdot 0 + c_1 \cdot 0 + ... + c_l \cdot N + c_{l+1}\cdot 0 + ... +c_{N-1}\cdot 0 = c_l \cdot N\]
\[c_l=\frac{1}{N}\sum _{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)e^{-j2\pi ln/N}\]

Example 1: Determine the Fourier coefficients of the signal \(x(n)=\cos(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi}{3}n)+\sin(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi}{5}n)\).

\[x(n)=\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi}{3}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\Big(-\frac{2\pi}{3}\Big)n}}+\frac{1}{2j}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi}{5}n}}-\frac{1}{2j}e^{\displaystyle{j\Big(-\frac{2\pi}{5}\Big)n}}\]
\[e^{\displaystyle{j\Big(-\frac{2\pi}{3}\Big)n}}=e^{\displaystyle{j\Big(-\frac{2\pi}{3}+2\pi\Big)n}}=e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{4\pi}{3}n}}, \quad e^{\displaystyle{j\Big(-\frac{2\pi}{5}\Big)n}}=e^{\displaystyle{j\Big(-\frac{2\pi}{5}+2\pi\Big)n}}=e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{8\pi}{5}n}}\]
(5)\[\Rightarrow x(n)=\frac{1}{2j}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi}{5}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi}{3}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{4\pi}{3}n}}-\frac{1}{2j}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{8\pi}{5}n}}\]
Let \(x_1(n)=\cos(2\pi\frac{1}{3}n)\), \(x_2(n)=\sin(2\pi\frac{1}{5}n)\).
The fundamental periods of \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) are \(=3\) and \(5`respectively. The fundamental period of :math:`x(n)\) is the smallest common factor of these two funsamental periods such that \(N=15\). It follows that
(6)\[x(n)=\sum_{k=0}^{14}c_k e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi}{15}kn}}\]

The only nonzero terms in (6) are the ones in (5)

\[j\frac{2\pi}{5}n=j\frac{2\pi}{15}3n \Rightarrow k=3, c_3=-j/2 \quad;\quad j\frac{2\pi}{3}n=j\frac{2\pi}{15}5n \Rightarrow k=5, c_5 =1/2\]
\[j\frac{4\pi}{3}n=j\frac{2\pi}{15}10n \Rightarrow k=10, c_{10}=1/2 \quad;\quad j\frac{8\pi}{5}n=j\frac{2\pi}{15}12n \Rightarrow k=12, c_{12} =j/2\]

Power density spectrum of a periodic signal¶

The power of a periodic signal \(x(n)\) with the fundamental period \(N\) can be calculated using the formula

\[P_x=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} |c_k|^2\]

This formula can be derived from the formula of the power of a general signal in the following steps:

  • Step 1: The power of a general signal \(x(n)\) is defined as
\[P_x=\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{2N+1}\sum_{n=-N}^{N} |x(n)|^2\]

According to this formula the power of the signal is equal to the average value of \(|x(n)|^2\) over the entire discrete time domain. In case of a periodic signal, since the fundamental period of the signal is repeating itself, it is enough to take the average of \(|x(n)|^2\) over the fundamental period as follows

(7)\[P_x=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}|x(n)|^2\]
  • Step 2: (7) can be rearranged using the following 2 identities:
\[|x(n)|^2=x(n)\cdot {x(n)}^*\]
(8)\[{x(n)}^*=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{c_k}^*e^{-j2\pi kn/N}\]

The derivation of (8) is as follows:

\[\begin{split}x(n) &= \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}c_k e^{j2\pi kn/N } \\ &= \sum _{k=0}^{N-1}c_k\lbrace \cos(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k }{N}n) + j\sin(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k }{N}n)\end{split}\]

For any two complex numbers \(z_1, z_2 \in \mathbb{C}\), \(((z_1+z_2)^*={z_1}^* + {z_2}^*)\). In order to prove this let \(z_1=a_1 + jb_1\) and \(z_2=a_2 + jb_2\).

(9)\[\begin{split}\Rightarrow (z_1 + z_2)^* &=(a_1 + a_2 + j(b_1 + b_2))^* \\ & = a_1+a_2-j(b_1 + b_2)=(a_1-jb_1)+(a_2-jb_2)={z_1}^* + {z_2}^*\end{split}\]

Using this result the complex conjugate of \(x(n)\) can be calculated as

(10)\[{x(n)}^*=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\Big\lbrace c_k \cos\Big(\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big) + jc_k\sin\Big(\frac{2\pi k }{N}n\Big) \Big\rbrace ^*\]

Let \(c_k=a_k +jb_k\). Then (10) becomes:

(11)\[\begin{split}{x(n)}^* &=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\Big\lbrace (a_k + jb_k)\cos\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big) + j(a_k + jb_k)\sin\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k }{N}n\Big) \Big\rbrace ^* \\ &=\sum _{k=0}^{N-1}\Big\lbrace \Big( a_k\cos\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n \Big) - b_k\sin\Big( \displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n \Big) \Big)+j\Big(b_k\cos\Big( \displaystyle\frac{2\pi k }{N}n \Big) + a_k\sin\Big( \displaystyle\frac{2\pi k }{N}n \Big) \Big) \Big\rbrace ^* \\ &=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\Big(a_k\cos\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big) - b_k\sin\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big)\Big)-j\Big(b_k\cos\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big) +a_k\sin\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big)\Big) \\ &=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\Big(a_k -jb_k\Big)\cos\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big)-j\Big(a_k - jb_k\Big)\sin\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big) \\ &=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\Big(a_k - jb_k\Big)\Big(\cos\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big)-j\sin\Big(\displaystyle\frac{2\pi k}{N}n\Big)\Big) \\ &=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{c_k}^* e^{-j2\pi kn/N}\end{split}\]
  • Step 3: Using equation (8), the power of a periodic signal \(x(n)\) can be written as:
(12)\[\begin{split}P_x &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{c_k}^* e^{-j2\pi kn/N} \\ &=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{c_k}^*\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)e^{-j2\pi kn/N} \\ &=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{c_k}^*c_k=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}|c_k|^2\end{split}\]

Using equation (12) the power of the signal in Example 1 can be determined as follows:

\[P_x = |c_3|^2 + |c^5|^2 + |c_{10}|^2 + |c_{12}|^2 = 4\Big(\frac{1}{2}\Big)^2 = 1\]

Example 2: Let \(x(n) = 2 + 2\cos\displaystyle\frac{\pi}{4}n + \cos \displaystyle\frac{\pi}{2}n + \displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\cos \displaystyle\frac{3\pi}{4}n\). This signal can be decomposed into \(4\) components as \(x_1(n)=2\), \(x_2(n)=2\cos\displaystyle\frac{\pi}{4}n\), \(x_3(n)=\cos\displaystyle\frac{\pi}{2}n\), \(x_4(n)=\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\cos\frac{3\pi}{4}n\).

\[x_1(n)\text{ is a constant and doesn't contain a frequency component.}\]
\[x_2(n)=2\cos\Big(2\pi \frac{1}{8}n\Big) \Rightarrow f_2=1/8 \Rightarrow N_2 = 8\]
\[x_3(n)=\cos\Big(2\pi \frac{1}{4}n\Big) \Rightarrow f_3=1/4 \Rightarrow N_3 = 4\]
\[x_4(n)=\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\cos\Big(2\pi \frac{3}{8}n\Big) \Rightarrow f_4=3/8 \Rightarrow N_4 = 8\]

The smallest common factor of \(N_1, N_2, N_3\) is \(N=8\) which is the fundamental period of the signal.

\(x(n)\) can be written in form of equation (1) as follows:

\[x_1(n)=2e^{\displaystyle{j0n}}=2e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi 0}{8}n}} \Rightarrow k=0,\quad \boxed{c_0=2}\]
\[\begin{split}x_2(n)&=2\Big( \frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{\pi}{4}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{-j\frac{\pi}{4}n}}\Big)=e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{\pi}{4}n}}+e^{\displaystyle{j(-\frac{\pi}{4}+2\pi)n}} \\ &=e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{\pi}{4}n}}+e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{7\pi}{4}n}}=e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi k_1}{8}n}}+e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi k_2}{8}n}}\\ &\Rightarrow k_1=1,\quad \boxed{c_1 = 1}, \qquad k_2=7, \quad \boxed{c_7=1}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}x_3(n)&=\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{\pi}{2}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{-j\frac{\pi}{2}n}}=\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{\pi}{2}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j(-\frac{\pi}{2}+2\pi)n}} \\ &=\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{\pi}{2}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{3\pi}{2}n}}=\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi k_3}{8}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi k_4}{8}n}} \\ &\Rightarrow k_3 = 2,\quad\ \boxed{c_2 = 1/2}, \qquad k_4 = 6, \quad \boxed{c_6 = 1/2}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}x_4(n)&=\frac{1}{2}\Big( \frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{3\pi}{4}n}}+\frac{1}{2}e^{\displaystyle{-j\frac{3\pi}{4}n}}\Big)=\frac{1}{4}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{3\pi}{4}n}}+\frac{1}{4}e^{\displaystyle{j(-\frac{3\pi}{4}+2\pi)n}} \\ &=\frac{1}{4}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{3\pi}{4}n}}+\frac{1}{4}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{5\pi}{4}n}}=\frac{1}{4}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi k_5}{8}n}}+\frac{1}{4}e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi k_6}{8}n}}\\ &\Rightarrow k_5=3,\quad \boxed{c_3 = \frac{1}{4}}, \qquad k_6=5, \quad \boxed{c_5=\frac{1}{4}}\end{split}\]

Since the signal doesn’t have the component \(c_4e^{\displaystyle{j\frac{2\pi 4}{8}}}\), \(\boxed{c_4 = 0}\).

In case of real valued periodic signals, for even valued \(N\), \(k \in \lbrace 0, 1, ..., N/2 \rbrace\) and for odd valued \(N\), \(k \in \lbrace 0, 1, ..., (N-1)/2 \rbrace\) completely describes the power density spectrum of the signal. In order to prove this we can use the fact that the Fourier series of \(x(n)\) and \({x(n)}^*\) are equal to each other for a real signal.

\[\begin{split}x(n)=\sum_{k=-(N-1)}^{0}c_ke^{\displaystyle{j2\pi k n/N}}&=c_0 + c_{-1}e^{\displaystyle{j2\pi (-1)n/N}} + ... \\ &+ c_{-(N-2)}e^{\displaystyle{j2\pi (-(N-2))n/N}} + c_{-(N-1)}e^{\displaystyle{j2\pi (-(N-1))n/N}}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}{x(n)}^*=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{c_k}^*e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi k n/N}}&={c_0}^* + {c_1}^*e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi (1)n/N}} + ... \\ &+ {c_{(N-2)}}^*e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi (N-2)n/N}} + {c_{(N-1)}}^*e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi (N-1)n/N}}\end{split}\]
\[x(n) = {x(n)}^* \Rightarrow c_0 = {c_0}^*, \quad c_{-1} = {c_1}^*, . . ., c_{-(N-2)} = {c_{(N-2)}}^*, \quad c_{-(N-1)} = {c_{(N-1)}}^*\]
(13)\[c_{-k} = {c_k}^* , \quad \forall k \in {0, ..., N-1}\]

An immediate result of equation (13) is as follows:

(14)\[\begin{split}&|{c_k}^*| = |c_k| = |c_{-k}|=|c_{N-k}| \\ &\angle {c_k}^* = \angle c_{-k} =\angle c_{N-k}= -\angle c_k\end{split}\]

In equation (14) the periodicty of \(c_k\) and the relationship \(\angle {c_k}^*=-\angle c_k\) are used. The periodicity of \(c_k\) implies that \(\forall k\), \(c_k = c_{k+N}\). This can be shown using equation (2) as follows:

\[c_{k+N}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi (k+N)n/N}}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x(n)e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi kn/N}}e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi Nn/N}}\]
\[e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi n}} = \cos(2\pi n) - j \sin(2\pi n) = 1\]
\[\Rightarrow c_{k+N}=c_k\]

In Example 2 \(x(n)\) is a real-valued signal and \(N=8\). Therefore \(|c_k|^2\) for \(k \in \lbrace 0, 1, 2,3, 4 \rbrace\) completely describes the power density spectrum.

The above plot shows one period of the signal \(x(n)\). The fundamental period of the signal is \(N=10\). The Fourier coefficients of \(x(n)\) can be calculated using equation (2). For \(n \in [3,7]\), \(x(n)=0\). Therefore equation (2) becomes:

\[\begin{split}c_k&=\frac{1}{10}\Big ( x(0)e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi 0k/10}} + x(1)e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi 1k/10}} + x(2)e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi 2k/10}} + x(8)e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi 8k/10}} + x(9)e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi 9k/10}} \Big) \\ &=\frac{1}{10}\Big( 1+ e^{\displaystyle{-j\pi k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{-j\pi 8k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{-j\pi 9k/5}} \Big) \\ &=\frac{1}{10}\Big( 1 + e^{\displaystyle{-j\pi k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{j2\pi k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{j\pi k/5}} \Big) \\ &= \frac{1}{10}e^{\displaystyle{-j2\pi k/5}}\Big( 1+ e^{\displaystyle{j\pi k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{j2\pi k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{j3\pi k/5}} + e^{\displaystyle{j4\pi k/5}} \Big)\end{split}\]

Let \(r=e^{j\pi k/5}\). Then

\[\begin{split}c_k &= \frac{1}{10}r^{-2}\sum_{p=0}^{4}r^p = \frac{1}{10}r^{-2}\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 5 & \mbox{ if $ k=0(r=1)$}\\ \displaystyle\frac{1-r^5}{1-r} & \mbox{ if $k \neq 0(r \neq 1)$} \end{array} \right. \\ &=\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 1/2 & \mbox{ if $ k=0$}\\ \frac{1}{10}r^{-2}\displaystyle\frac{r^{5/2}(r^{-5/2}-r^{5/2})}{r^{1/2}(r^{-1/2}-r^{1/2})} & \mbox{ if $k \neq 0$} \end{array} \right. =\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 1/2 & \mbox{ if $ k=0$}\\ \frac{1}{10}\displaystyle\frac{e^{-j\pi k/2}-e^{j\pi k/2}}{e^{-j\pi k/10}-e^{j\pi k/10}} & \mbox{ if $k \neq 0$} \end{array} \right. \\ &=\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 1/2 & \mbox{ if $ k=0$}\\ \frac{1}{10}\displaystyle\frac{-2j\sin(\pi k /2)}{-2j\sin(\pi k /10)} & \mbox{ if $k = 0$} \end{array} \right. =\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 1/2 & \mbox{ if $ k=0$}\\ \frac{1}{10}\displaystyle\frac{\sin(\pi k /2)}{\sin(\pi k /10)} & \mbox{ if $k \neq 0$} \end{array} \right.\end{split}\]

Since \(x(n)\) is a real valued periodic signal \(c_k\) for \(k \in [0, 5]\) completely describes the power spectrum of the signal

The Fourier Transform¶

The Fourier transform of an aperiodic energy signal is defined as

\[X(\omega)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}x(n)e^{\displaystyle{-j\omega n}}\]
